I'm a capitalist who is for drastic wealth redistribution. Let me explain.

In heated online debates, capitalists and socialists often seem to be shouting across a canyon. One side defends markets, innovation, and private enterprise as the engine of progress. The other highlights inequality, exploitation, and the need for systemic change to ensure everyone has basics like water, clothes, food, and housing.

We're all talking past each other. Capitalism is designed to commoditize and cheapen goods over time—sometimes to the point of near-zero marginal cost—while socialism (in its purest forms) risks derailing that process through premature redistribution that chokes the innovation pipeline. Socialists falsely attribute near-free commodity benefits of capitalism as THEIR contribution.

Tech bros understand the robots are going to take us to the promised land if we play our cards right. Everyone should agree that being a garbage man is a crap-can job that no human being should have to do if it can be automated. It can. Destruction of crap-can commodity jobs is capitalist. Pretending the former holders of crap-can jobs can "just get new jobs" when 80% of the available jobs are definitely going to disappear in the next 20 years is NOT capitalist: it's monopolist. Capitalists claim "new jobs always get created." Yeah, they do... 20 years from now after several generations of people are jobless for 20 years and have no option but handouts and drugs. A preemptive solution to this train wreck is not socialist: it preserves capitalism. It's capitalist.

## The End of Crap-Can Jobs

A huge chunk of today's "valid jobs" involve repetitive, low-skill manual labor that squanders human potential. Garbage collection, basic construction, and food production fall into this category. Tech bros see the commodity benefits on the horizon, but for anyone down on their luck in life, abundance can't come too soon. 

But if we force the promised land to come too soon, it won't come at all. People outside the tech industry see the trend toward abundance, and they want commodity pricing RIGHT NOW. Often, they've read about Marx in fine art school and believe products and services can be commoditized at the snap of a finger as long as policy changes, which is utter nonsense as proven in every real-world experiment.

**Robots as the new garbage men—and builders.** By 2050, it's highly plausible that robots will grab, toss, and handle every bag of trash at your curb. In construction, [3D printing (additive manufacturing) is already demonstrating rapid, automated home-building](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2949822826006465). Early 3D printed homes are revealing growing pains, but given enough R&D time, the structures will become livable at fractions of today's contstruction cost. Robotic arms and gantry systems layer materials to create structures faster and cheaper than traditional methods, with projects like 3D-printed neighborhoods and even hotels underway. Humanoid robots who can set up the gantries will hit the market in less than five years. This isn't sci-fi; it's scaling now, reducing labor needs dramatically and it will improve precision and sustainability in mass-market production.

**Food abundance via cultured meat.** On the protein front, [cultured (lab-grown) meat faces real hurdles](https://www.foodengineeringmag.com/articles/103734-cultured-meat-faces-old-and-new-obstacles)—high costs, scaling bioreactors, regulatory pushback in some U.S. states, and consumer acceptance—but the trajectory is toward commoditization. Advances in serum-free media, larger bioreactors (e.g., 20,000+ liters), continuous production, and cell densities are driving costs down sharply. Projections show potential for price parity with conventional or organic meat in the coming decade under optimistic scaling. Follow that trend to 2050, and a petri-dish-grown burger is probably 1/10th the price of a farm-raised burger.

Nearly 80% of current "jobs" could be automated feasibly. This is the New Industrial Revolution: across every sector, its hallmark will be job displacement into new industries or, more often, an upstream shift from low-skill/low-wage work to higher-value trade-school-trained artisan work. Crucially, in THIS revolution, the mass job loss will be followed by a blackout period BECAUSE the only jobs left are high-skill positions that require retraining. In the gap, there will be an unprecedented dirth of human jobs available. We've never seen a wave of millions-to-billions of metal laborers who don't sleep enter human labor markets in history, thus the best econ texts do not prepare us for what's coming.

Governments and capitalists will be hungry to hide the job loss reality with tried-and-true accounting tricks e.g. manipulating CPI and unemployment metrics to make it seem like all is quiet on the western front. But the people will know when they can't afford chicken breast anymore. The people will know when 30 years of their hard labor won't cover a mortgage anymore.

## Capitalism's Self-Cannibalizing Genius

Socialists and capitalists alike mistake free water, free food, and free housing as socialist proposals. They are the RESULTS of commoditization through capitalism. Capitalism doesn't just tolerate commoditization—it thrives on it until competition becomes irrelevant. Historical precedent: In 1850, clean public drinking water and bathrooms were rare luxuries in many places. Today, they're near-free in much of the developed world. Capitalism optimized its "long tail" out of existence through abundance.

The root cause of the feedback loops starts in the energy sector. Human workers build solar plants, battery plants, and robot manufacturing plants. The first wave of robots take over the maintenance of solar and battery plants to power the production of more robots. The positive feedback loop begins, yielding more robots each year at near-zero continuous cost for humanity. The robots take up basic manual labor in food and housing at $0 wages. 

Basic calories, vegetables, cultured proteins, and shelter trend toward near-free as production scales. Entrepreneurs can't compete in the commodity end of these sectors anymore. Who wants to pay a fellow human being to solve a solved problem? The long tail of commodity goods and services become free. Bespoke artisan work e.g. furniture, hand-crafted guitars, premium chef experiences, or custom architecture—is the higher ground at which humanity continues to thrive in a capitalist system, but only if unskilled laborers train up, and that takes time. Anyone can have a tract house for free. If you want a Hobbit house in the side of a hill, hire the artisans who specialize in Hobbit houses. There will be several competitors in bespoke home design and hand-crafting because their high-skill labor is no longer wasted in mass tract home construction, eventually.

Making water and food and shelter free isn't the death of capitalism; it's the success story. Markets drive the R&D, efficiency, and investment needed for factories, bioreactors, and robotic fleets. Along the way, socialist will take credit for the commodity boons of capitilism, citing anything free as THEIR idea. But if we rush toward socialism, corruption will surely strangle the very systems that manifest the robots that manifest the abundance. Socialists aren't wrong to want basic water, food, and shelter to become free—they're just too greedy to see that demanding it NOW prevents us from achieving it through hard work in the coming decades.

## The Ownership and Distribution Question

But if the laborer trains up and becomes an artisan, in this new world, their purchasing power is still crushed one trillion fold by the rise of titans of industry who command giant robot armies capable of owning 99.999% of the world's productivity with no obligation to let non-robot-owners in on the action. Who SHOULD own the robots and the means of commoditized production? Should it be the tycoons alone? I don't think most capitalists realize that if their artisan wages compete against the aristocracy's spending power once they have armies of robots as their ATM, the best artisan wages will be 1/10th of poverty-level adjusted for inflation in that world. A few billionaires controlling robot armies whether for labor or military purposes is dystopian. Pure government monopoly risks the same stagnation, corruption, and tyrannical danger.

The answer lies in EARLY distributed ownership—treating advanced productive assets (robotics fleets and the energy infrastructure used to sustain them) as public utilities while preserving markets for humanity. Gains must circulate broadly, not pool in private vaults. This sustains the system without teetering into Communism. Human labor reallocates to artistry, expertise, innovation, and care, which expands the "expert class," and their spending power is supplemented by endowed ownership in automation infrastructure inherited as an inalienable right to pursue property. They cannot, in fact, pursue property once inflation prices their piddly wages out of all goods and services, which means distributed ownership of auomated labor is a core necessity under the Constitution.

In a nation prosperous enough to buil 330 million robots across a dozen companies, for instance, each citizen can theoretically participate in the gains of that robot labor, at least in part. The entrepreneurs who invested in the original energy and manufacturing infrastructure will naturally want to retain full ownership of that robot's productive capability, but they will need to recognize that putting human workers out of most jobs will prevent them from being future customers, sending an otherwise capitalist system into a deflationary death spiral for everyone who doesn't own robots. 

Government must redistribute not the final wealth generated by robots (which often results in con games via the tax system). Instead, they should favor upfront ownership stake in  the means of the labor. Building robotic labor needs to mean "building a public utility." You can keep SOME of the ownership, but some of it belongs to the people automatically. 

This should not be a tax, since taxes give governments ample leeway to cyphen the funds to their own selfish desires. It should be dividends from an ownership stake in the existence of each working robot. Companies deploying robots for manual labor should have a legally-required dividend payout to EVERY citizen tied to a portion of their robotic labor force' productivity. If there are 330 million Americans in the country, and there are 330 million robots, each citizen should own a fraction each robot's total annual labor output. The more robots a company builds, the more output they provide for both the company AND the citizens who inherit stock in them. 

Distributed robotic labor dividends preserve the necessary circulation of money in a capitalist system. It can exist in the form of an index fund whose value is accrued via dividends paid out by a fraction of revenue from a company's annualized robotic labor force. Every company that deploys robots for manual labor pays the dividend into the fund. Every born citizen starts with one share in the fund. This dividend bypasses the graft machine through normal taxation in DC. Direct wealth distribution, straight from the public utility to the pockets of citizens at the point of creation is a necessity to SAVE capitalism. It's not socialism. This the only thing that could keep socialism at bay to protect the artisan side of the market.

## Cherish the System, Steer It Wisely

Fifty years out, with solar-powered robotic loops intact, food, housing, and basics can be near-free without collapsing into dystopia. We won't have full communism as long as we avoid extremes: monopolistic hoarding on one end, or hasty nationalization on the other. There are three options: the giant productivity gains of the robot era will accrue in private companies, in DC, or partially distributed into the hands of humanity by default. I believe option 3 is the only one that doesn't end in bloody conflicts. We should recognize that capitalism is INTENDED to render some sectors post-competitive. We should cherish competitive markets where they drive value (luxuries, services, new frontiers), automate the drudgery, and ensure broad access to the fruits of productivity.

The robots are coming. When they arrive, we will probably not survive a failure to distribute their productivity gains. The only question is whether the abundance goes to a few or to everyone.
